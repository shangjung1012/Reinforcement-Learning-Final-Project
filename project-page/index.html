Abstract

Routing retrieval actions as an offline contextual bandit

Fixed retrieval strategies in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) apply the same action—BM25, dense, hybrid, or query rewrite—to every incoming query, regardless of difficulty or domain. We reframe per-query retrieval selection as an offline contextual-bandit problem: for each query, a lightweight policy observes lexical and BM25-confidence features and selects the retrieval action expected to maximize a cost-aware reward combining Recall@5 and MRR minus retrieval-call penalties.

We evaluate on two BEIR benchmarks—SciFact (scientific claim verification) and NFCorpus (biomedical/nutrition retrieval)—using full-corpus offline replay with 600 training and 300 test queries per dataset. The selective policy outperforms the best fixed action on both datasets, with bootstrap 95% CIs strictly above zero, and transfers across domains without retraining. The claim is intentionally narrow: cost-aware evidence retrieval, not generated-answer EM/F1, not LLM fine-tuning.

Problem

Fixed retrievers cannot adapt to query difficulty.

A single retrieval action is rarely optimal for every query. On SciFact, a scientific claim-verification corpus, hybrid retrieval and keyword rewrites improve coverage. On NFCorpus—a short-title biomedical corpus—one-call BM25 or dense retrieval often suffices. The cost of extra retrieval calls (rewrite API, additional index lookups) is non-trivial and should be weighed against expected evidence gain.

We frame this as a one-step offline contextual bandit over retrieval actions, training from logged retrieval outcomes stored as a full-information replay table. The reward combines retrieval-quality signals and a call-cost penalty, so the policy must learn not only which action is best, but also when the gain justifies the extra cost.

Method

Offline routing policy over retrieval actions

State

Lexical query features (length, IDF mass, stop-word ratio) and BM25 retrieval-confidence statistics (top-1 score, score gap, score variance).

Actions

BM25, dense (DPR-style), hybrid BM25+dense, keyword rewrite + BM25. Gemini-backed actions are expensive extensions evaluated separately.

Policy

Direct-method action-value estimators (Linear Thompson Sampling, LinUCB adapted for offline replay). Model selected via 5-fold CV on training reward.

Evaluation

Full-information held-out replay, bootstrap 95% CIs, Doubly Robust OPE, three-seed robustness sweep, and audit-trail artifact checks.

Policy objective

Policy behavior

The learned policy adapts its action mix by domain.

SciFact action mix

BM25 35.7% Dense 23.0% Hybrid 41.3%

Hybrid and keyword rewrite (49.0% of queries) are frequent—scientific claim queries benefit from multi-index evidence and lexical expansion.

NFCorpus action mix

BM25 44.7% Dense 55.3%

Hybrid is not selected on held-out NFCorpus. Short biomedical titles are well-served by single-index retrieval; rewrite appears on 31.3% of queries.

Budget sweep (λ)

At λ=0.06, SciFact keeps +0.039 utility delta at only 1.05 calls/query—essentially matching one-call cost. NFCorpus already achieves its best constrained utility at exactly 1.00 call/query.

Cross-Dataset Generalization

Policies trained on one domain transfer to another.

We test zero-shot transfer by applying a policy trained on one BEIR dataset to a completely different target domain—without any retraining. Both transfer directions produce positive reward deltas over the target's own train-best fixed action, suggesting the feature-based policy learns domain-agnostic routing signals.

The SciFact→NFCorpus transfer incurs extra retrieval calls (1.597 vs 1.000), indicating that some SciFact-learned preferences for hybrid retrieval do not pay off in the biomedical domain. The NFCorpus→SciFact direction transfers cleanly at 1.000 calls/query.

Diagnostics

Evidence that supports and bounds the claim

Selected-action replay

Linear Thompson Sampling delta vs. train-best: +0.005 on SciFact and +0.027 on NFCorpus under selected-action feedback only.

Replay regret

LinUCB selected-feedback regret is 82.1 vs 67.4 full-information direct on SciFact, and 43.2 vs 29.6 on NFCorpus—confirming that partial-feedback learning is strictly harder than full-replay.

OPE coverage warning

SciFact IPS error rises from 0.127 (uniform logging) to 0.268 (sparse train-best logging). DR-based estimates are used as the primary offline evaluator.

3-seed robustness

Selective win rate is 0.667 on both datasets across three seeds. Mean reward deltas: +0.021 (SciFact) and +0.025 (NFCorpus).

Limitations & Claim Boundary

What this work does and does not claim

📄

Retrieval-stage evidence only

We measure Recall@5 and MRR, not downstream EM/F1 or generation quality. The QA pilot is exploratory and small-scale.

🗂️

Full-information replay table

Training uses pre-computed rewards for all actions on each query. This is offline supervised learning with a bandit framing—not true online bandit learning from deployed logs.

🧪

Simulated OPE

Off-policy evaluation uses simulated behavior logging, not real user interaction data. IPS estimates degrade under sparse logging.

🔤

Semantic features analysis-only

BERT/embedding features were explored in ablation but remain outside the main policy. The production policy uses only lexical and BM25-confidence features.

📊

Small corpora

SciFact (5,183 docs) and NFCorpus (3,633 docs) are both small BEIR datasets. Scaling to Wikipedia-scale corpora is future work.

🤖

No LLM fine-tuning

Deliberately out of scope: we do not fine-tune any retriever or reader model. All improvements come from routing, not representation learning.
